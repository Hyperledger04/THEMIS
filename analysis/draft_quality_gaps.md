# Draft Quality Gaps

_Why LexAgent drafts feel weak — not because of intelligence, but because of missing abstractions, missing review layers, and assembly failures._

_Each gap is scored: Impact (P0/P1/P2), Difficulty (Low/Medium/High), Confidence (HIGH/MEDIUM)._

_Gap references are used in `draft_delta.md` and `priority_roadmap.md`._

## Gap 1 — Document Assembly Leakage

**Impact:** P0 | **Difficulty:** Medium | **Confidence:** HIGH

**What it is:**
A court filing is a packet of distinct documents with specific ordering rules:
```
Index
Memo of Parties
Main Pleading (complaint / plaint / petition)
Prayer
List of Documents (with exhibit labels)
Affidavit-in-Evidence (separate document)
List of Witnesses
Vakalatnama (separate, filed at counter)
```

The draft node generates one monolithic text blob. The docx_writer writes one .docx file. The filing packet concept does not exist anywhere in the codebase.

**Where it breaks:**
- `nodes/draft.py:289–415` — single LLM call, single string output
- `tools/docx_writer.py:24–141` — writes one .docx, no sub-document concept

**What is missing:**
- A `FilingPackageBuilder` that knows what sub-documents a given matter type requires
- A second LLM call (or structured prompt section) for the affidavit-in-evidence
- A `list_of_documents.docx` generated from the exhibit registry
- A `lawyer_notes.docx` containing the client summary and risk assessment (separated from the filing)

## Gap 2 — Lawyer Working Notes Bleed Into the Filing

**Impact:** P0 | **Difficulty:** Low | **Confidence:** HIGH

**What it is:**
The most damaging single bug. `base_system.md:42–46` explicitly instructs the LLM to append a Plain English Summary and Risk Assessment after a `---` separator. `docx_writer.py:65–79` writes the entire `draft_output` string to the .docx without parsing or stripping this separator.

Every court filing generated by LexAgent currently contains:
- "Plain English Summary: The complainant has a strong case..."
- "⚠ HIGH RISK: signature mismatch may be contested if accused provides evidence..."
- "Matter ID: M-4B4626CE | Generated by LexAgent | Phase 5 draft — verify citations before filing"

These are lawyer working notes. They have no place in a court filing. A court officer reading the filed document would see the system's internal analysis — which could embarrass the advocate and, in adversarial proceedings, potentially be used by opposing counsel.

**Where it breaks:**
- `lexagent/prompts/base_system.md:42–46` — instructs LLM to include notes in draft_output
- `lexagent/tools/docx_writer.py:65–79` — writes entire draft_output without stripping
- `lexagent/tools/docx_writer.py:99–107` — adds "Generated by LexAgent | Phase 5 draft" footer

**Fix:** Parse draft_output at the `---` separator in `write_docx()`. Write only the content before the separator to the filing .docx. Write everything after the separator to a separate `lawyer_notes.docx`.

## Gap 3 — No Formal Court Header

**Impact:** P0 | **Difficulty:** Low | **Confidence:** HIGH

**What it is:**
Every Indian court filing begins with a formal header identifying: the court, the bench designation, and the geographic location. This is the first thing a court officer, judge, and opposing counsel reads. It establishes jurisdiction and the document's status.

Indian district courts:
```
IN THE COURT OF THE CHIEF JUDICIAL MAGISTRATE,
GAUTAM BUDH NAGAR AT GREATER NOIDA
```

High Courts:
```
IN THE HIGH COURT OF JUDICATURE AT ALLAHABAD
(Lucknow Bench)
```

Supreme Court:
```
IN THE SUPREME COURT OF INDIA
CRIMINAL ORIGINAL JURISDICTION
```

**What LexAgent produces:**
```
Legal Document          ← generic Heading 1
Petitioner v. Respondent ← wrong party labels
[jurisdiction, italic]  ← free text, not formatted as a court header
```

**Where it breaks:**
- `tools/docx_writer.py:43–60` — title_para uses `matter_type.title()` ("Legal Document", "Complaint", "Writ Petition")
- `tools/docx_writer.py:49–55` — party labels from dict keys: `parties.get("plaintiff") or parties.get("petitioner")` — uses writ/civil labels for criminal matters

**Fix:** Read court name from SOUL.md `## Court Preferences` (set during setup wizard). Use party labels from a matter_type → label map, not from dict key guessing.

## Gap 4 — No Standalone Affidavit Generation

**Impact:** P0 | **Difficulty:** Medium | **Confidence:** HIGH

**What it is:**
In Indian court proceedings, the affidavit-in-evidence is a separate document from the main pleading. The complaint says what happened (pleading). The affidavit says "I swear that what the complaint says is true" and adds the evidentiary record (sworn testimony + exhibit references). For S.138 NI Act cases tried under Section 143's summary procedure, the affidavit IS the examination-in-chief — the complainant may not be called to give oral testimony if the affidavit is complete.

LexAgent generates a verification clause at the end of the complaint body (`"I, [name], do hereby verify that the contents of this complaint are true..."`). This is a pleading attestation, not an evidence affidavit. They are legally distinct.

A complete affidavit-in-evidence contains:
- 12–14 numbered paragraphs restating all complaint facts in first person
- Reference to each exhibit by its court label (EX-CW1/A, EX-CW1/B…)
- Statement that all attached documents are true copies
- Deponent's signature block
- Oath commissioner's stamp and signature

**Where it breaks:**
- `nodes/draft.py` — single LLM call, no sub-document concept
- `tools/docx_writer.py` — writes one .docx, no affidavit section
- `lexagent/graph.py` — no affidavit node in the graph

**Fix:** Add a second LLM call within the draft node (or a new AffidavitNode) that generates the affidavit as a separate string. Write it to `affidavit_evidence.docx` via a separate `write_affidavit_docx()` function.

## Gap 5 — Exhibit Reference Inconsistency

**Impact:** P1 | **Difficulty:** Low | **Confidence:** HIGH

**What it is:**
Indian court exhibit labels follow a specific format:
- District courts: `EX-CW{witness_number}/{sequential_alpha}` — e.g., EX-CW1/A (first exhibit of Court Witness 1)
- High Courts: `Annexure P-{number}` — e.g., Annexure P-1 (first petitioner's annexure)
- Some courts: `Ex.P-1`, `Exhibit-A`

LexAgent uses "Annexure A", "Annexure B" — generic labels that are neither district court format nor HC format. Worse, the exhibit labels in the complaint body, the affidavit, and the list of documents must be identical. If the LLM generates them independently in each section, they will drift.

**Where it breaks:**
- `nodes/draft.py` — no exhibit registry; LLM chooses labels inline
- No cross-reference mechanism between complaint body and affidavit

**Fix:** Build an exhibit registry from the facts before drafting:
```python
exhibit_registry = {
    "invoice_001": "EX-CW1/A",
    "return_memo_1": "EX-CW1/B",
    "demand_notice": "EX-CW1/C",
    "postal_receipt": "EX-CW1/D",
    ...
}
```
Inject this registry into the draft prompt with the instruction: "Use only these exhibit labels. Do not invent labels." The registry is then the same for the complaint body, the affidavit, and the list of documents.

The exhibit label format itself should come from SOUL.md court preferences (set at setup wizard).

## Gap 6 — Missing Procedural Liability and Skill Coverage

**Impact:** P1 | **Difficulty:** Low | **Confidence:** HIGH

**What it is:**
Two related problems:

**6a — No S.141 conditional block:**
Section 141 NI Act extends criminal liability to "every person who, at the time the offence was committed, was in charge of and was responsible to the company for the conduct of the business." Without a S.141 paragraph, only the cheque signatory is pinned. For matters where the accused is a proprietor, partner, or director, S.141 is mandatory.

The intake node does not extract `accused_entity_type`. The draft prompt has no conditional block that fires S.141 when the accused is a legal entity or when a proprietor/partner is identified. This is a pure logic gate — not an LLM task.

**6b — Missing S.138 complaint skill:**
`templates/legal_notice_s138.md` is an excellent demand notice template. A complaint is structurally different:
- Different document type (court pleading vs lawyer's letter)
- Different party labels (Complainant/Accused vs client/noticee)
- Different structure (numbered court paras vs letterhead format)
- Requires affidavit, witness list, list of documents
- Requires S.141 conditional block
- Different prayer (conviction and fine vs payment demand)

There is no `skills/s138_complaint.md`. The intake node loads the legal_notice skill for S.138 matters, which is the wrong structural reference.

**Where it breaks:**
- `lexagent/state.py` — no `accused_entity_type` field
- `nodes/intake.py` — does not ask about accused entity type
- `nodes/draft.py` — no S.141 conditional injection
- `lexagent/skills/` — no dedicated s138_complaint skill
- `lexagent/templates/` — legal_notice_s138.md is used for complaints (wrong)

## Gap 7 — No Pleading Schema Enforcement

**Impact:** P1 | **Difficulty:** High | **Confidence:** HIGH

**What it is:**
The structure of every draft is 100% LLM-generated. Nothing in the pipeline checks:
- "Does this S.138 complaint have a CAUSE OF ACTION paragraph?"
- "Does it have a JURISDICTION paragraph citing S.142(2)?"
- "Does it have a LIMITATION paragraph citing S.142(b)?"
- "Does the PRAYER include the specific sentencing norm (twice the cheque amount)?"
- "Are the mandatory sections in the correct order?"

The review node (`nodes/review.py:44–79`) only checks word count and citation verification. A complaint with no Cause of Action paragraph, no Jurisdiction paragraph, and no proper Prayer would pass the review node without any warning.

**Why this matters procedurally:**
- A complaint without an explicit Cause of Action para can be returned by the registry as deficient
- A complaint without S.142(2) jurisdiction grounding is vulnerable to a preliminary objection on jurisdiction
- A complaint without S.142(b) limitation grounding is vulnerable to a preliminary objection on limitation
- These are standard pre-trial objections that experienced defence counsel will always raise

**Where it breaks:**
- `nodes/review.py:44–79` — section presence check does not exist
- `lexagent/templates/` — templates are narrative hints, not section schemas

**Fix (minimum viable):** In the review node, after draft generation, check for the presence of required section keywords:
```python
REQUIRED_SECTIONS = {
    "s138_complaint": ["CAUSE OF ACTION", "JURISDICTION", "LIMITATION", "PRAYER"],
    "writ_petition": ["GROUNDS", "PRAYER", "VERIFICATION"],
    "bail_application": ["PRAYER"],
    "plaint": ["CAUSE OF ACTION", "JURISDICTION", "LIMITATION", "PRAYER", "VERIFICATION"],
}
```
Flag missing sections as structural errors, not just warnings.

**Fix (robust):** Implement Pleading Schema Registry (see `court_draft_architecture.md` Layer B) — YAML schemas per document type that enumerate required sections, their ordering rules, and their dependencies on specific facts.
